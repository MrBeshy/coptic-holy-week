Holy Week

The Great Pascha

Holy Week in the Coptic Orthodox Church is the most solemn period of the liturgical year. Each day carries specific rites, readings, and prayers. The faithful are expected to attend services, fast strictly, and enter fully into the commemorations of the Passion, death, and resurrection of Christ.

The Musical Shift

On Palm Sunday, the Divine Liturgy is sung in the festive tunes of the great feasts. These bright, expansive melodies are the same ones heard at Christmas, Epiphany, and the major saints' days throughout the year.

When the Palm Sunday liturgy ends, those tunes stop. Every service from Sunday evening through Bright Saturday is chanted exclusively in the mournful tunes of the sorrowful season. The Coptic liturgical tradition has two distinct musical registers for these seasons, and the shift between them is immediate and unmistakable. Before a word about mourning is spoken, the congregation hears it.

The Fast

The fast of Holy Week is among the strictest in the Coptic tradition. Many faithful abstain from all food and water from midnight through the end of each day's services, with Good Friday observed as a complete fast until the ninth hour. The fast is not merely discipline; it is participation. The body joins the mourning of the week alongside the prayers and the chanting.

At midnight on Resurrection Sunday the mournful tunes cease and the festive melodies return without transition. The Church does not ease into the resurrection; the announcement breaks the silence and the bright tunes follow immediately. The contrast with the preceding week is intentional and complete.

Palm Sunday

Sunday of Hosanna

Palm Sunday commemorates the triumphal entry of Christ into Jerusalem. The faithful gather bearing palm branches, olive branches, and green reeds. During the service they weave the palms into small crosses, a practice observed across Coptic communities worldwide. The deacons vest in red stoles, the colour of feasts and royal celebration.

The Four Gospel Accounts

A distinctive feature of the Coptic Palm Sunday liturgy is that all four Gospel accounts of the entry into Jerusalem are read aloud in sequence:

  • Matthew 21:1–17, "Hosanna to the Son of David!"
  • Mark 11:1–11, "Blessed is the kingdom of our father David."
  • Luke 19:28–44, "If these should keep silent, the stones would immediately cry out."
  • John 12:12–19, "Fear not, daughter of Zion; behold, your King is coming."

After each reading the congregation chants the Hosanna responses, the festal hymns particular to this Sunday. The liturgy is celebrated in full festive mode. This is the last feast music the congregation will hear until midnight the following Sunday.

The Turning of the Stoles After the Palm Sunday liturgy concludes, the deacons remove their red stoles and replace them with black before the congregation departs. This is the formal beginning of Holy Week mourning. From this moment through Holy Saturday, all clergy and deacons serving at the altar will wear black. The festive tunes have ended. The sorrowful season has begun.

The General Funeral

The reason the stoles change immediately at the close of Palm Sunday, rather than at the start of the following service, is that the Church celebrates a general funeral directly after the liturgy. This is a communal commemoration of all the faithful departed, observed on this day each year. Because the funeral rite requires the black stoles of mourning, the transition from the festive red of Palm Sunday to the black of Holy Week happens in that moment, on the same day, within the same gathering. The shift is not simply a liturgical notation; it is enacted in full view of the congregation as the Church moves from the joy of the entry into Jerusalem directly into the grief of burial.

Covenant Thursday

Thursday of the Covenant

Covenant Thursday commemorates the Last Supper: the institution of the Eucharist, the washing of the disciples' feet, and the arrest of Christ in Gethsemane. The service is one of the most theologically dense of the week, holding the gift of the Eucharist and the beginning of the Passion in the same liturgical space.

The Lakan: Washing of the Feet

In many Coptic churches, the Lakan rite is observed on this night. The bishop or senior priest re-enacts Christ's washing of the disciples' feet, described in John 13. This ceremony is one of the more visually striking moments of the Holy Week services and draws a large attendance.

The readings of Covenant Thursday trace the full arc of the night: the Passover meal prepared, the bread and cup given with the words "Do this in remembrance of me," the prediction of the betrayal, the prayer in Gethsemane, and the arrest. By the close of the service the liturgical week has fully entered its most sorrowful hours.

The fast continues. The stoles remain black. The congregation leaves knowing that Good Friday begins before dawn.

Good Friday

Friday of the Crucifixion

The Good Friday service is called the Pascha. It runs from approximately 8am to 5pm and is the longest single service of the liturgical year. The faithful observe a strict fast throughout, abstaining from all food and water from midnight until the service concludes. Attendance is expected for the full duration.

The Agpeya Hours

The structure of the Pascha follows the hours of the Agpeya, the Coptic Book of Hours. The congregation moves through the full day of the Crucifixion in real time, from the early morning trial to the burial at evening.

The Twelve Passion Gospel Readings

Woven through the Agpeya hours are twelve consecutive Gospel readings covering the complete Passion narrative, from Gethsemane to the sealing of the tomb. Between readings the congregation responds with prostrations and the mournful antiphons particular to this day. The service concludes at approximately 5pm.

Bright Saturday

The Great Sabbath

Bright Saturday consists of two separate services. The first, Apocalypse Night, begins at 11pm on Good Friday and runs through the early hours of Saturday morning. The second, the Saturday Night Liturgy, begins at 7pm Saturday evening and concludes at midnight with the breaking of the 55-day Lenten fast.

Apocalypse Night · Friday 11pm

Apocalypse Night opens with the chanting of the entire Book of Revelation. The congregation gathers at 11pm and remains for the complete reading, which is followed by a full Divine Liturgy. The Apocalypse is read in this context not as a warning but as an assurance: the victory of the Lamb is already declared. The Church reads the end of the story while still sitting in the silence of the tomb.

The Saturday Night Liturgy · Saturday 7pm

The congregation returns Saturday evening for the night liturgy, which begins at 7pm. This is the final service of the 55-day Lenten season. The deacons remain in black stoles. The altar curtain remains drawn. The mournful tunes of Holy Week continue through the service.

At midnight the liturgy reaches its culmination. The Eucharist is offered and the faithful receive communion, breaking the 55-day fast. This communion is the formal end of the Great Lent. Immediately after the liturgy concludes, the priest makes the announcement of the Resurrection.

"Watchman, what of the night? Watchman, what of the night?
The watchmen said, "The morning comes, and also the night." Isaiah 21:11–12

Resurrection Sunday

The Feast of Feasts

After the Saturday Night Liturgy concludes, the priest makes the announcement: Christos Anesti, Christ is Risen. The congregation responds: Alithos Anesti, Truly He is Risen. The 55-day fast has been broken with communion. The mournful tunes of Holy Week do not return. The festive melodies of the great feasts resume immediately and completely.

The Stoles Return to Light Following the announcement, the deacons remove their black stoles and vest in white or gold. The stoles that were changed at the close of Palm Sunday are now restored. The mourning of Holy Week is complete. The feast of the Resurrection has begun.

Resurrection Sunday

Resurrection Sunday is observed as a day of communal celebration. Many Coptic churches organize gatherings at the church or at a local park, with activities and events for children and adults. Families who spent the week in fasting, prayer, and long services now spend the day together in rest and joy. It is the deliberate other side of Holy Week: where the week was marked by solemnity and discipline, the day is marked by food, fellowship, and festivity.

"Death is swallowed up in victory.
O Death, where is your sting?
O Hades, where is your victory?" 1 Corinthians 15:54–55

The greeting Christos Anesti is exchanged throughout the fifty days of the Resurrection season that follow, until Pentecost. Holy Week is complete.
